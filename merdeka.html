Ah Ma's story
 Growing up The War Kampung Life Merdeka

Singapore was changing—
Vehicles drove around blaring
Political slogans and rallies,
Cries of “Merdeka!”

There was a lot of talk of independence from the British,
A lot of excitement in town.

It was 1957,

and Malaya was on the cusp of self-rule.

Every day, people gathered excitedly
Around our neighbours’ black and white TV

Chinese students locked themselves in a school And wouldn’t listen to parents or police

We heard of riots, clashes with authorities. We were not to go out after curfew.

A principal of a famous girls’ school had acid thrown at her face—

I could not imagine it. In our little kampung, we always respected one another.

There was one Mr Lee Kuan Yew
who was talking about a better future for all.

I liked him—
he could speak English, Hokkien and Malay
And he had many ideas for improving our lives.

I hoped there would be a good future for my children.

It was, after all, the swinging 60s --

Gramophones and the Beatles,
Young people hung out in groups
in skinny jeans and miniskirts.

But one day, we heard on TV-- Singapore had separated from Malaysia.

I was so confused.

We were a separate country now--
What did that mean?

My husband’s employers offered him a job in Britain.

We could migrate there, become British—
But how could I survive there,
when I could not even speak English?

We stayed.

The country flourished--
We got our own commercial airline,

flag

and national anthem.

The children got jobs in respectable companies,
Went to parties and gatherings on the weekends.

In 1979, the Government offered us
a 4 room flat in Ang Mo Kio.
It had electricity and running water.

I was so happy –
No more carrying buckets from the standpipe!

My house had its own tiled bathroom, with a metal door
Ceramic tiles on the walls

I was so proud of it.

We have lived many happy years here.
