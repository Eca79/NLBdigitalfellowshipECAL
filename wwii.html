Ah Ma's story
 Growing up The War Kampung Life Merdeka

When I was 12, the war broke out.
In the kampung, we heard there was a war,
but it still seemed far away.

Then one night, on 8 December 1941,
we heard strange noises—planes rushed overhead,
and there were huge explosions in the distance.

We were very scared, but there was nowhere to go,
so we stayed where we were in the house.

The next day we heard:
Seletar and Tengah airbases,
Chinatown,
Raffles Place
and Keppel Harbour
had been bombed.

The war had come to Singapore.

Over the days, the shelling and bombing got worse.

The air raid sirens blared throughout the day,
The planes came

I quickly lay down inside a drain
And kept very still
Until the danger passed.

Then, on 15 Feb 1942,
the first day of the Lunar New Year,
we heard the Japanese had won.

Singapore was now Syonan-to.

Light of the South,
but our hearts were darkened with grief and fear.

All the girls cut our hair short,
wore baggy clothes, dressed as boys.

We were so scared.
If soldiers came,
our parents made us hide.

On moonlit nights, we wore white
And black on moonless nights,
So as to not be seen,
to be invisible.

To be invisible was safe.

People disappeared.

People we knew
Reported for registration, as they were told.
...They never came back.

Luckily in my family there was only mum,
my little brother who was still a child,
and me.

But as time went by,
there was less and less food.

We ate a lot of sweet potato,
which we grew near our house.

My mother didn’t allow me to go out much.

But we saw people decapitated in the streets,
Their heads left as a warning.

I saw a baby bayoneted.
I had nightmares for weeks.

How can people be so cruel?

The war years passed.

The war ended, the British returned,
but nothing was the same.

We struggled to rebuild our lives,
Mourned lost friends and neighbours,
But we were thankful we had survived
When so many had not.

At least, we no longer had to hide or bow, And life was slowly returning to normal—

But now, “normal” was different.

Strikes were everywhere.

There was an energy in the air
That had not been there before.

It was the energy of the desire for self-rule.

In my life, too, normal was different.

One day when I was 17, a matchmaker came to see me with a photo.

His older sister had seen me and
wanted to introduce us.

At first, I said no. I didn’t like talking to strangers.

But the matchmaker persisted, and I gave in. We had our first date at Great World City.

I took some relatives with me, for security.
--I didn’t even know this man!

He looked a bit shocked to see so many people,
And I laughed to myself that now I had scared him.

Of course, he invited everyone to sit down and eat something.

As we talked, I thought he seemed like a decent man.

He was quiet and gentle, and he worked with the British—
Above all, I felt he would be a good, dependable provider.

In two weeks, we were engaged.

And within two months, I was married.
