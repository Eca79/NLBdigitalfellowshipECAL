Ah Ma's story
 Growing up The War Kampung Life Merdeka

My husband had lost his parents at an early age, and his sister was all the family he had left.

So we moved in with my sister-in-law ’s family, to a house in Upper Serangoon.

They were Peranakan.

There I learned to help with the family kueh business.

My family grew, and soon, we had to move.

Tampines was our new home,
which we shared with another family.
It had an attap roof, and when it rained,
we put buckets all over to catch the leaks.
Although it was small, it was ours.

But the one window we had, was so narrow
That when the lady at the market
tried to sell me curtains, I laughed—
our window was only one foot wide.

So, when I had our fourth child, we moved again.

We “upgraded”: The house had a zinc roof and stilts,
and chickens and geese clucked around the yard.

My six children kept me busy:

  • Doris,
  • Jane,
  • Mary,
  • Lilian,
  • Rose,
  • Harry

We had little money.

My husband’s work as a clerk
earned him just enough to support us.

In the mornings,
I would fetch water from the standpipe

Boil water for the Kopi-O and rice porridge

Or make margarine and kaya sandwiches
for the children

Or oats with condensed milk.

Lunch was simple –
a soup, a tofu or vegetable,
one fish or pork dish.

On special days like Chinese New Year,
there was chicken or duck meat.

Time went on.

The family expanded,
but our income didn’t.

I had to feed more children with the same money…

Lunch became just porridge

with a piece of luncheon meat,

or half a fishcake.

I never thought we were poor,

at least we could afford the school fees.
We made sure they could all go to school.

Over the years, between preparing meals
and washing clothes,
feeding ducks
and making dresses...

my children grew up, one by one.
